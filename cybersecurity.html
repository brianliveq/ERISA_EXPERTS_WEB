Cybersecurity and Your Retirement Plan:
A Fiduciary Obligation, Not Just an IT Concern

The Department of Labor has made clear that protecting plan participant data is a fiduciary responsibility. Plan sponsors who ignore cybersecurity risk don't just face data breaches — they face personal liability under ERISA.

What Is It

Retirement plan data is a high-value target — and fiduciaries are responsible for protecting it.

Retirement plans hold sensitive personal and financial data for thousands of participants. In 2021, the Department of Labor issued its first formal cybersecurity guidance for ERISA-covered plans, establishing that fiduciaries have an obligation to ensure strong cybersecurity practices are in place across the plan and its service providers.

This means cybersecurity is no longer solely an IT or vendor issue — it is a governance issue. Plan sponsors and committees that fail to evaluate and monitor cybersecurity practices are exposed to regulatory scrutiny and, in the event of a breach, to claims of fiduciary breach under ERISA.

Why This Matters

Cybersecurity risk is fiduciary risk.

A breach affecting plan participants is not just a public relations problem. It is a failure of fiduciary oversight — one that regulators and plaintiffs' attorneys increasingly treat as actionable under ERISA.

Common sources of cybersecurity exposure for plan sponsors include:

  • Failure to evaluate service providers' cybersecurity programs during selection and ongoing monitoring
  • No written cybersecurity policy governing plan data access and handling
  • Weak identity verification and access controls for participant accounts
  • Lack of incident response procedures specific to retirement plan data
  • Insufficient due diligence on third-party vendors with access to plan systems
  • No annual review or audit of cybersecurity practices against DOL guidance

Meeting the DOL's cybersecurity standards is not optional — it is part of a prudent fiduciary process.

Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

Is cybersecurity a fiduciary obligation under ERISA?

Yes. The Department of Labor's 2021 cybersecurity guidance clarified that the duty of prudence under ERISA Section 404 extends to cybersecurity. Plan fiduciaries are responsible for ensuring that adequate cybersecurity practices are in place — both within their own organization and across the service providers that handle plan data. Failure to do so can constitute a breach of fiduciary duty.

What did the DOL's 2021 cybersecurity guidance require?

The DOL issued three documents: Cybersecurity Program Best Practices (directed at plan service providers), Tips for Hiring a Service Provider with Strong Cybersecurity Practices (directed at plan sponsors and fiduciaries), and Online Security Tips (directed at plan participants). Together, they establish that fiduciaries must evaluate service provider cybersecurity as part of the selection and monitoring process, and maintain formal cybersecurity programs for plan-related systems.

What should plan fiduciaries ask their service providers about cybersecurity?

Fiduciaries should ask service providers to confirm they have a formal cybersecurity program, conduct annual third-party audits, maintain cyber liability insurance, have a documented incident response plan, and can provide evidence of past security practices. Service contracts should also include cybersecurity representations and breach notification requirements. These questions should be part of initial due diligence and ongoing monitoring.

What happens if a retirement plan suffers a data breach?

A breach affecting plan participant accounts or data may trigger notification obligations, DOL investigation, and participant claims under ERISA. If fiduciaries cannot demonstrate that they followed a prudent process in selecting and monitoring service providers, they may face personal liability for losses resulting from the breach. Having documented cybersecurity due diligence practices in place before a breach occurs is the most important step fiduciaries can take.

How often should a plan's cybersecurity posture be reviewed?

The DOL recommends that plan fiduciaries review cybersecurity practices at least annually, and also whenever a service provider relationship changes, new plan technology is adopted, or a security incident occurs. Reviews should be documented in the same way as other fiduciary decisions — creating a clear record that the plan committee took its obligations seriously.
