The Independent Fiduciary:
Accountability That Doesn't Stop at Advice

When fiduciary responsibility is formally transferred in writing, plan sponsors gain more than protection — they gain a partner who is legally accountable for every decision made on their behalf.

What Is It

An independent fiduciary doesn't just advise.
It assumes responsibility.

Under ERISA, a fiduciary is any person or entity that exercises discretionary authority or control over a plan's management, assets, or administration. An independent fiduciary goes a step further — it is formally named, engaged in writing, and legally accountable for the decisions it makes on behalf of the plan.

Most plan consultants, advisors, and record keepers provide services without formally accepting fiduciary status. An independent fiduciary steps into that gap — absorbing the legal responsibility that would otherwise remain with the plan sponsor, its officers, and its committees.

Why This Matters

Fiduciary risk is not theoretical.

It affects the people responsible for plan oversight, the way decisions are made, and the level of exposure carried by sponsors, boards, committees, and executives.

Common sources of fiduciary risk include:

  • Inconsistent committee oversight
  • Weak documentation of fiduciary decisions
  • Failure to monitor providers or investments appropriately
  • Delayed action on known issues
  • Unclear allocation of responsibilities
  • Overreliance on conflicted or bundled service relationships

A prudent process does more than improve administration. It helps protect the sponsor.

Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

What does an independent fiduciary actually do?

An independent fiduciary formally accepts fiduciary status under ERISA in writing, then exercises discretionary authority over the functions assigned in the engagement. This may include overseeing plan investments, managing prohibited transaction exemptions, making benefit determinations, or serving as named fiduciary for the plan as a whole. Unlike an advisor, the independent fiduciary does not merely recommend — it acts, and it is legally accountable for those actions.

How is an independent fiduciary different from a plan advisor or consultant?

A plan advisor or consultant typically provides guidance, analysis, and recommendations — but does not accept fiduciary liability for the decisions made. An independent fiduciary formally assumes that liability in writing. The key distinction is accountability: when something goes wrong, the independent fiduciary is legally responsible; an advisor generally is not.

When is hiring an independent fiduciary required under ERISA?

ERISA does not always require an independent fiduciary, but it is required or strongly advisable in several situations: prohibited transaction exemptions that mandate independent oversight, plan terminations involving the selection of an annuity provider, disputes or conflicts of interest among existing fiduciaries, and circumstances where the plan sponsor itself has a conflict with the plan's interests. Outside of mandated situations, plan sponsors often engage independent fiduciaries proactively to reduce personal liability exposure.

Can an independent fiduciary be held personally liable?

Yes. That is precisely what distinguishes an independent fiduciary from an advisor. By formally accepting fiduciary status under ERISA, the independent fiduciary assumes legal accountability for the decisions within the scope of its engagement. This is why selecting a qualified, experienced independent fiduciary firm — not simply the least expensive option — is itself a fiduciary decision that remains with the plan sponsor.

What types of plans can benefit from an independent fiduciary?

Any ERISA-governed plan can benefit, including defined contribution plans (such as 401(k) plans), defined benefit pension plans, employee stock ownership plans (ESOPs), health and welfare plans, and voluntary employee beneficiary associations (VEBAs). The appropriate scope of engagement varies by plan type and the specific risks involved.
