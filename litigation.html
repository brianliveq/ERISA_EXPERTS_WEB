ERISA Litigation:
What Plan Sponsors Need to Understand Before It Happens

ERISA class action lawsuits against plan sponsors have surged over the past decade — targeting not just large corporations, but mid-size employers and their executives. Understanding what triggers litigation, and what courts look for, is the first step toward meaningful protection.

What Is It

ERISA gives plan participants the right to sue. Fiduciaries bear the risk.

ERISA Section 502 gives plan participants, beneficiaries, and the Department of Labor the right to bring civil actions against plan fiduciaries for breaches of their duties. These claims can result in personal liability — requiring the fiduciary to restore losses to the plan out of their own assets.

Unlike many corporate liability contexts, ERISA fiduciary liability is not automatically covered by general corporate indemnification. Plan sponsors, HR leaders, CFOs, and committee members who serve as plan fiduciaries can face direct personal exposure if they fail to meet the statute's standards of prudence and loyalty.

Why This Matters

Litigation risk follows fiduciary failure.

Courts do not require plaintiffs to prove bad intent — only that fiduciaries failed to follow a prudent process. A plan sponsor who cannot document their decision-making is already at a disadvantage before trial begins.

Common triggers for ERISA fiduciary litigation include:

  • Excessive or undisclosed plan fees paid to record keepers or advisors
  • Imprudent investment selection or retention of underperforming funds
  • Failure to monitor plan investments and service providers on an ongoing basis
  • Conflicts of interest in service provider selection
  • Inadequate documentation of fiduciary committee decisions
  • Use of proprietary investment products that benefit the plan's service provider

The best defense in ERISA litigation is a well-documented, consistently followed fiduciary process — established before any dispute arises.

Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

Who can be sued under ERISA for a fiduciary breach?

Any person or entity that is a fiduciary under ERISA can be named in a fiduciary breach claim. This includes the plan sponsor, individual committee members, HR leaders and CFOs who exercise discretionary authority over the plan, and outside service providers who have accepted fiduciary status. Liability is personal — it is not automatically absorbed by the employer as a corporate entity.

What is the standard courts use to evaluate fiduciary conduct?

Courts apply a "prudent expert" standard — asking whether the fiduciary acted with the care, skill, prudence, and diligence that a knowledgeable person familiar with retirement plan management would use in similar circumstances. Importantly, courts focus on process, not just outcomes. A fiduciary who followed a thorough, documented process may prevail even if the plan suffered losses, while one who made the right decision for the wrong reasons may still face liability.

Can an employer indemnify committee members against ERISA liability?

Employers may indemnify fiduciaries for certain ERISA-related claims, and fiduciary liability insurance is available. However, ERISA prohibits plan assets from being used to indemnify a fiduciary against liability for a breach of fiduciary duty. Indemnification must come from company assets, and coverage under standard D&O policies varies. Fiduciaries should not assume they are protected without reviewing their specific coverage terms.

How does hiring an independent fiduciary reduce litigation risk?

When a plan sponsor formally delegates fiduciary authority to an independent fiduciary in writing, the legal responsibility for covered decisions transfers to that firm. This means that if a participant challenges those decisions in litigation, the independent fiduciary — not the plan sponsor — is the proper defendant. The plan sponsor's residual obligation is limited to the prudence of the hiring decision itself, which is a far narrower exposure.

What is the statute of limitations for ERISA fiduciary breach claims?

Under ERISA Section 413, fiduciary breach claims must generally be brought within six years of the breach, or three years from the date the plaintiff had actual knowledge of the breach — whichever is earlier. In cases of fraud or concealment, the six-year period may be extended. Because the limitations period can run from the date of the breach rather than discovery, past decisions that were never properly documented remain a source of ongoing exposure.
